2026 Federal Tax Brackets in Canada

Understanding the five federal income tax brackets for the 2026 tax year, how marginal rates work, and what the basic personal amount means for your tax return.

Estimated reading time: 7 minutes

Every year, the Canada Revenue Agency indexes federal income tax brackets and credit amounts to inflation. For the 2026 tax year, brackets increased by approximately 2.7% over the 2025 thresholds. This means slightly more of your income is taxed at lower rates compared to 2025.

How Progressive Tax Brackets Work

Canada uses a progressive tax system. You do not pay the top rate on all your income. Instead, each portion of your income that falls within a bracket is taxed at that bracket’s rate.

For example, if your taxable income is $100,000 in 2026:

  • The first $58,924 is taxed at 15% = $8,838.60
  • The remaining $41,076 ($100,000 − $58,924) is taxed at 20.5% = $8,420.58
  • Total federal tax before credits: $17,259.18

Your marginal rate (the rate on your next dollar of income) is 20.5%. Your average rate (total tax divided by total income) is 17.3%.

Basic Personal Amount (BPA)

The basic personal amount is a non-refundable tax credit that ensures Canadians pay no federal tax on a base amount of income. For 2026, the BPA is $16,579 (up from $16,129 in 2025).

However, the BPA is income-tested. If your net income exceeds $177,882 (the bottom of the fourth federal bracket), the BPA is gradually reduced. It reaches a minimum of $14,538 for incomes at or above $260,207.

The BPA credit is calculated at the lowest federal rate (15%), so the maximum federal credit value is $16,579 × 15% = $2,486.85 for most taxpayers.

CPP and EI in 2026

Employment income is also subject to payroll deductions:

  • CPP contributions: 5.95% on employment earnings between $3,500 and $68,500, to a maximum of $4,034.10. Self-employed individuals pay both portions at 11.9%, to a maximum of $8,068.20.
  • EI premiums: 1.63% on insurable earnings up to $68,500, to a maximum of $1,117.79. Self-employed individuals do not participate in EI.

Quebec residents contribute to the Quebec Pension Plan (QPP) and the Quebec Parental Insurance Plan (QPIP) instead of CPP and EI, with different rates and maximums.

Provincial Tax Brackets

Federal brackets are only part of the picture. Each province and territory has its own set of brackets and rates. Combined federal-provincial marginal rates can exceed 50% in several provinces. See our personal tax rates page for a full provincial breakdown or use the tax estimator to see your specific situation.

Important: Taxable vs Total Income

The bracket thresholds apply to taxable income, not gross income. Deductions like RRSP contributions, union dues, and child care expenses reduce your taxable income. Your marginal rate is the key number for planning additional income or deductions.
