Corporate Tax Instalments Explained

How corporate tax instalments work for Canadian-controlled private corporations, including due dates, calculation methods, and what happens if you miss a payment.

Estimated reading time: 5 minutes

Unlike employees who have taxes withheld at source, corporations must pay their income tax through quarterly instalments. If your corporation owes more than $3,000 in federal tax for the current year (or either of the two preceding years), you are required to make instalment payments.

Instalment Due Dates

For corporations with a December 31 year-end, quarterly instalments are due:

  • March 31
  • June 30
  • September 30
  • December 31

If your corporation’s year-end is not December 31, instalments are due on the last day of the 3rd, 6th, 9th, and 12th months of the tax year. For example, a corporation with a June 30 year-end pays on September 30, December 31, March 31, and June 30.

How Instalment Amounts Are Calculated

The CRA sends instalment reminders based on one of three methods, and you can choose whichever produces the lowest total instalment obligation:

  1. Prior-year method: Pay ¼ of the previous year’s tax each quarter.
  2. Current-year estimate method: Pay ¼ of your estimated current-year tax each quarter.
  3. Second prior-year method (first two quarters): For the first two instalments, pay ¼ of the tax from two years prior. For the last two instalments, pay enough so that total instalments equal the lesser of the prior-year method and the current-year estimate.

Most small corporations use the prior-year method for simplicity, but the current-year method can help manage cash flow if you expect lower income.

Interest and Penalties

If instalments are late or insufficient, the CRA charges instalment interest at the prescribed rate (currently 6%, compounded daily) from the due date of each instalment to the balance-due date. Instalment interest is not tax-deductible for the corporation.

If your instalment payments total less than the required amount, instalment interest is calculated on the shortfall for each quarter. Even if your year-end payment covers the total tax owing, you can still face instalment interest for earlier quarters.

Provincial Instalments

Most provinces align with the federal instalment threshold and schedule. However, Quebec and Alberta administer their own corporate tax systems and may have slightly different rules. Always confirm with provincial tax authorities.

Instalment Planning Tip

Set aside tax reserves in a separate bank account. A common rule of thumb is to reserve 15–25% of corporate revenue for tax, depending on your province and income level. Speak with your accountant to determine the right percentage for your corporation.
