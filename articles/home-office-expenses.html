Home Office Expenses for Employees & Self-Employed

What you can claim for working from home in 2026. The rules differ significantly between employees and self-employed individuals.

Estimated reading time: 6 minutes

If you work from home, your eligibility to claim home office expenses depends on whether you are an employee or self-employed. The rules are substantially different, and confusing the two can lead to disallowed deductions.

For Employees

To claim home office expenses as an employee, you must meet all of the following conditions:

  • Your employer required you to work from home (this condition is more strictly enforced post-pandemic).
  • You worked from home more than 50% of the time for a period of at least four consecutive weeks.
  • You have a signed Form T2200, Declaration of Conditions of Employment, from your employer.
  • Your home office is your principal place of work or used exclusively for work on a regular and continuous basis.

What Employees Can Claim

Eligible expenses include a prorated portion (based on the workspace area as a percentage of home square footage) of:

  • Rent (not mortgage interest or principal)
  • Electricity, heating, and water
  • Home internet
  • Maintenance and minor repairs to the workspace
  • Office supplies used directly in your work

Employees cannot claim mortgage interest, property taxes, home insurance, or capital cost allowance on the home.

For Self-Employed Individuals

Self-employed individuals have a significantly broader range of deductible home office expenses, provided the workspace is used exclusively for business on a regular basis and is your principal place of business.

What Self-Employed Can Claim

In addition to the expenses available to employees, self-employed individuals may also claim a prorated portion of:

  • Mortgage interest (but not principal)
  • Property taxes
  • Home insurance
  • Capital cost allowance (CCA) on the building (with caution—this can affect the principal residence exemption)

Principal Residence Exemption Warning

Claiming CCA on your home can jeopardize the principal residence exemption when you sell. The portion of the home used for business may not qualify for the exemption, and claiming CCA can complicate the calculation. Consult a tax professional before claiming CCA on your home.

Calculating the Business-Use Percentage

The standard method is to divide the square footage of your workspace by the total finished square footage of your home. If the workspace is not used exclusively for business (e.g., a dining table used as a desk during the day), the space may not qualify for the self-employed deduction at all.

If the workspace is used for both personal and business purposes, you must also prorate by the time used for business (e.g., 8 hours per day / 24 hours = 33.3%).

Record-Keeping Requirements

Keep all utility bills, rent receipts, mortgage statements, and insurance documents. The CRA requires you to retain records for six years from the end of the tax year they relate to. If you claim home office expenses, have a clear floor plan or measurement of your workspace ready in case of review.
