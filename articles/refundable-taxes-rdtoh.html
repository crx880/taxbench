Refundable Taxes, RDTOH & Capital Dividends in a CCPC

How refundable taxes work inside a Canadian-controlled private corporation, including NERDTOH, ERDTOH, the capital dividend account, and the general rate income pool.

Estimated reading time: 9 minutes

Canadian-controlled private corporations (CCPCs) are subject to a unique system of refundable taxes designed to achieve integration—the principle that business income should attract roughly the same total tax whether earned personally or through a corporation. Understanding how this system works is essential for owner-managers who want to optimize their compensation strategy.

The Small Business Deduction

The small business deduction (SBD) reduces the federal corporate tax rate on the first $500,000 of active business income earned by a CCPC. For 2026 the SBD rate is 9%, compared to the general corporate rate of 15%. Combined with the provincial small business rates, the total corporate tax on active business income up to the small business limit is typically between 9% and 14%, depending on the province.

The $500,000 small business limit is associated with a group of corporations and is reduced on a straight-line basis when a CCPC and its associated corporations have between $10 million and $15 million in aggregate taxable capital employed in Canada. The limit is also restricted for corporations earning significant passive investment income—the business limit is gradually reduced where the associated group earns more than $50,000 in aggregate investment income in the preceding year, and is eliminated entirely at $150,000.

Refundable Taxes in a CCPC

CCPCs pay additional refundable taxes on certain types of income. Unlike the corporate tax on active business income, these amounts are held in notional CRA accounts and are refunded to the corporation when it pays taxable dividends to its shareholders. This mechanism ensures that corporate and personal tax systems integrate properly.

There are two separate RDTOH (Refundable Dividend Tax On Hand) accounts:

NERDTOH (Non-Eligible Refundable Dividend Tax On Hand)

NERDTOH tracks the refundable portion of Part I tax that a CCPC pays on its aggregate investment income (AII). Aggregate investment income includes interest, rent, royalties, and net taxable capital gains, but excludes Canadian dividends.

The NERDTOH amount is calculated as 30.67% of aggregate investment income. Specifically, the refundable portion under section 129(3) is 30.67% of the lesser of:

  • The corporation’s aggregate investment income for the year; and
  • The corporation’s taxable income minus the amount eligible for the small business deduction.

This 30.67% rate is designed to roughly equalize the corporate and personal tax on investment income. When the corporation pays non-eligible dividends to shareholders, the shareholders include the dividend income at the non-eligible dividend gross-up rate (15% in 2026) and claim the corresponding dividend tax credit. The NERDTOH is refunded to the corporation at a rate of $1 for every $2.61 of non-eligible dividends paid.

ERDTOH (Eligible RDTOH)

ERDTOH tracks Part IV tax that a CCPC pays on dividends received from non-connected corporations, primarily eligible dividends from public companies and certain private corporations that elect to pay eligible dividends.

Part IV tax is generally 38.33% of the portfolio dividends received. This is a refundable tax added to the ERDTOH account. The purpose is to prevent individuals from using a CCPC as a conduit to defer or avoid personal tax on dividend income.

ERDTOH is also refunded when the corporation pays taxable dividends, but crucially it is refunded when eligible dividends are paid. The refund rate is $1 for every $2.61 of eligible dividends paid. The refund first comes from ERDTOH if the corporation has an ERDTOH balance and pays eligible dividends.

How to Recover RDTOH by Paying Dividends

RDTOH balances are refundable only when the corporation pays taxable dividends. The refund mechanism operates as follows:

  • Paying eligible dividends: The corporation receives a dividend refund equal to the lesser of its ERDTOH balance and 38.33% of the eligible dividends paid. If there is still a dividend refund entitlement remaining after ERDTOH is exhausted, the refund comes from NERDTOH.
  • Paying non-eligible dividends: The corporation receives a refund from NERDTOH equal to 38.33% of the non-eligible dividends paid. ERDTOH cannot be drawn down by paying non-eligible dividends.

In practice, this means that a corporation with both NERDTOH and ERDTOH balances should generally pay eligible dividends first to recover the ERDTOH, and then decide whether to pay additional eligible or non-eligible dividends to recover the remaining NERDTOH.

RDTOH Traps to Avoid

Before 2019, there was a single RDTOH pool. Today’s split between NERDTOH and ERDTOH means that paying only non-eligible dividends will not trigger a refund of ERDTOH. Failing to plan for this can leave refundable tax locked inside your corporation indefinitely. Work with a CPA to structure dividend payments that efficiently recover both pools.

Capital Dividend Account (CDA)

The capital dividend account is a notional account that tracks the non-taxable portion of certain amounts received by a private corporation. The CDA allows a CCPC to distribute these amounts to shareholders as tax-free capital dividends. The math is important: only 50% of capital gains are taxable, so the other 50% is non-taxable and can be added to the CDA.

Key amounts that credit the CDA include:

  • Non-taxable portion of capital gains: When the corporation realizes a capital gain, 50% is included in taxable income (the taxable capital gain) and 50% is added to the CDA. When a capital loss is realized, the non-deductible portion (50%) reduces the CDA.
  • Life insurance proceeds: The net proceeds of a life insurance policy payable on the death of a person, less the policy’s adjusted cost basis, are credited to the CDA.
  • Capital dividends received from other corporations: If the corporation receives a capital dividend from another private corporation, that amount is added to its own CDA.

Capital gains from the sale of eligible small business corporation shares may qualify for the lifetime capital gains exemption (LCGE). In these cases, a portion of the non-taxable gain may not flow through the CDA in the same way—careful planning is required.

Capital Dividends: Tax-Free Distributions from the CDA

A capital dividend is a tax-free distribution to shareholders that is paid from the corporation’s CDA balance. Before paying a capital dividend, the corporation must elect under subsection 83(2) of the Income Tax Act by filing Form T2054 with the CRA. This election must be filed on or before the day the dividend is paid.

If the corporation pays a capital dividend that exceeds the actual CDA balance, the excess is subject to a penalty tax of 60% under Part III. The penalty can be avoided by electing to treat the excess as a taxable dividend. Accurate tracking of the CDA balance is therefore critical.

The CDA is a powerful planning tool for CCPC shareholders. It allows the tax-free withdrawal of funds that represent the non-taxable portion of capital gains and life insurance proceeds—money that, if withdrawn as salary or taxable dividends, would be subject to personal income tax.

General Rate Income Pool (GRIP)

The General Rate Income Pool is a notional account that tracks a CCPC’s capacity to pay eligible dividends. Eligible dividends entitle individual shareholders to the enhanced dividend tax credit (the gross-up is 38% for eligible dividends versus 15% for non-eligible dividends in 2026), resulting in a lower effective personal tax rate.

GRIP is generally increased by:

  • Active business income taxed at the general corporate rate (i.e., income above the small business limit or income for which the SBD was not claimed).
  • Eligible dividends received by the corporation that were not subject to Part IV tax.
  • Amounts designated by the corporation to flow through GRIP from other sources.

GRIP is reduced when the corporation pays eligible dividends. A CCPC cannot pay an eligible dividend unless it has a positive GRIP balance at the time of the dividend. If it pays an eligible dividend in excess of its GRIP, the excess portion is subject to Part III.1 tax.

Putting It All Together

Managing a CCPC’s tax attributes requires balancing several interconnected accounts:

  • NERDTOH accumulates from investment income and is refunded when non-eligible dividends are paid.
  • ERDTOH accumulates from Part IV tax on portfolio dividends and is refunded only when eligible dividends are paid.
  • CDA accumulates from the non-taxable portion of capital gains and life insurance proceeds and allows tax-free capital dividends.
  • GRIP accumulates from income taxed at the general rate and determines how much of a corporation’s dividends can be designated as eligible.

Properly sequencing withdrawals—for example, paying eligible dividends to recover ERDTOH, then capital dividends to distribute CDA, and then deciding between additional eligible or non-eligible dividends based on NERDTOH balances and the shareholder’s personal marginal rates—can significantly reduce the total tax burden for owner-managers.

The RDTOH, CDA, and GRIP rules contain complex interactions and anti-avoidance provisions. This article provides a general overview but does not substitute for professional tax advice tailored to your specific corporate structure and financial circumstances.
