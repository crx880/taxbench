RRSP vs TFSA Basics

A straightforward comparison of Canada’s two most popular tax-advantaged accounts, including 2026 contribution limits.

Estimated reading time: 6 minutes

RRSPs and TFSAs are the cornerstones of Canadian personal finance. Both shelter investment growth from tax, but they work in fundamentally different ways. Choosing the right one—or the right mix—depends on your income, your goals, and your tax bracket.

When to Prioritize Your RRSP

RRSPs shine when your current marginal tax rate is higher than the rate you expect to face in retirement. The immediate tax deduction at your high current rate, combined with tax-deferred growth, can produce substantial savings.

RRSPs are particularly effective for:

  • High-income earners in top tax brackets (marginal rates above 40%)
  • People without employer pension plans who need to build retirement savings
  • Those who want to use the Home Buyers’ Plan ($60,000 withdrawal limit) or Lifelong Learning Plan

When to Prioritize Your TFSA

The TFSA is more flexible and suits a wider range of situations. Because withdrawals are tax-free, the TFSA has no impact on income-tested benefits like the Canada Child Benefit, Old Age Security, or the Guaranteed Income Supplement.

TFSAs are especially valuable for:

  • Lower-income earners who do not benefit much from the RRSP deduction
  • Anyone who may need to access their savings before retirement
  • Seniors who want to shelter additional savings without triggering OAS clawback
  • Building an emergency fund that can still earn tax-free growth

The Ideal Strategy

For most Canadians, the best strategy is to contribute to both, weighted by income. A common rule of thumb:

  • Contribute enough to your RRSP to drop into a lower marginal tax bracket, if possible
  • Use your TFSA for the remainder of your savings
  • If you expect your income to rise significantly, consider prioritizing your TFSA in lower-income years and saving RRSP room for higher-income years

2026 Contribution Limits

  • TFSA: The annual TFSA dollar limit for 2026 is $7,000. If you have never contributed, your cumulative room since 2009 is approximately $102,000 (depending on residency and withdrawals).
  • RRSP: Your 2026 RRSP contribution room is 18% of your 2025 earned income, up to a maximum of $32,490, plus any unused room from prior years, minus any pension adjustments.

Over-contribution Penalties

Over-contributing to a TFSA triggers a penalty of 1% per month on the excess amount. For RRSPs, a $2,000 lifetime buffer is allowed, but amounts beyond that face a 1% monthly penalty. Always check your CRA My Account for your exact contribution room before making large deposits.
