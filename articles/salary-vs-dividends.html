Salary vs Dividends for Canadian Owner-Managers

How incorporated professionals should decide between paying themselves a salary or dividends from their corporation in 2026.

Estimated reading time: 10 minutes

One of the most common questions for Canadian incorporated professionals is how to pay themselves from their corporation: salary or dividends? The answer depends on your personal tax situation, retirement goals, and cash flow needs.

The Principle of Integration

Canada’s tax system is designed around the concept of integration. In theory, earning income personally versus through a corporation should produce roughly the same total tax result. The dividend tax credit mechanism and the small business deduction are intended to achieve this.

In practice, integration is imperfect. Whether salary or dividends leave more money in your pocket depends on your province, your personal income level, and whether you have access to the small business rate.

Key Considerations for Salary

Advantages

  • RRSP contribution room: Salary (but not dividends) generates RRSP contribution room equal to 18% of earned income, up to the annual maximum ($32,490 for the 2026 contribution year).
  • CPP contributions: Salary triggers CPP contributions, building your future retirement pension. For 2026, maximum employee CPP contributions are $4,034.10.
  • Deductibility for the corporation: Salary is a deductible expense for the corporation, reducing corporate taxable income.
  • Income splitting: A salary paid to a family member must be reasonable for the work performed, but can be an effective income-splitting tool when legitimate.

Disadvantages

  • CPP cost: You pay both the employee and employer portions of CPP (the employer portion comes from the corporation, but it is your corporation). Total CPP cost for salary of $68,500 is $8,068.20.
  • Payroll administration: Requires payroll remittances, T4 slips, and monthly or quarterly filings.
  • EI premiums: Generally, owner-managers who control more than 40% of voting shares are exempt from EI, but compliance is required.

Key Considerations for Dividends

Advantages

  • Lower initial tax rates: Eligible dividends (from income taxed at the general corporate rate) receive a higher dividend tax credit, while non-eligible dividends (from income taxed at the small business rate) receive a lower credit. Both are generally taxed at lower personal rates than salary.
  • No CPP: By taking dividends instead of salary, you avoid CPP contributions entirely. This may be advantageous if you believe you can earn a better return investing the savings yourself.
  • Simplicity: A dividend declaration requires a corporate resolution but no payroll remittances or source deductions.

Disadvantages

  • No RRSP room: Dividends do not count as earned income, so they generate no RRSP contribution room.
  • No CPP retirement pension: Choosing dividends means you will not accrue CPP benefits (unless you have other employment income).
  • Tax on retained earnings: If the corporation retains earnings and invests them, the investment income is taxed at high corporate rates (though a portion is refundable when dividends are paid).

A Common Approach: The Hybrid Strategy

Many owner-managers use a combination: pay enough salary to maximize RRSP room and CPP benefits (typically around $68,500 to $75,000—the YMPE for CPP and a level that generates meaningful RRSP room), and take any remaining income as dividends.

This approach secures retirement benefits while keeping marginal tax rates manageable on the salary portion. The exact optimal split depends on your province, your corporation’s income level, and whether the small business deduction applies.

Plan Ahead

Salary vs dividend decisions should be made before the end of each calendar year for salary and before the corporation’s year-end for dividends. Retroactive planning is limited. Consult a Canadian tax professional for advice specific to your situation.
