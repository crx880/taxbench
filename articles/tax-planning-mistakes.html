Common Canadian Tax Planning Mistakes

Avoid these frequent tax planning errors made by Canadian small business owners and individuals.

Estimated reading time: 8 minutes

Most Canadian taxpayers are not trying to be aggressive with their taxes. The mistakes that cost the most are often simple oversights, poor timing, or assumptions that turn out to be incorrect. Here are the most common ones—and how to avoid them.

1. Not Claiming All Available Credits and Deductions

Every year, Canadians leave money on the table by missing legitimate claims. The most commonly overlooked:

  • Medical expenses: You can claim expenses for any 12-month period ending in the tax year. Keep receipts for prescriptions, dental, vision, and travel for medical appointments. Premiums paid to private health plans also count.
  • Disability Tax Credit (DTC): Many people who qualify do not apply. The DTC is a non-refundable credit worth up to $1,400 federally, and it opens the door to the RDSP program.
  • Canada Workers Benefit: This refundable credit for low-income workers requires filing a return—it is not automatic.
  • Digital news subscriptions: The digital news subscription tax credit provides a 15% credit on qualifying subscriptions up to $500.

2. Mixing Personal and Corporate Expenses

For incorporated professionals, this is the single most common audit trigger. Using the corporate bank account for personal expenses, or running personal vehicle costs through the corporation without proper documentation, creates significant CRA risk.

If the corporation pays a personal expense, it should either be recorded as a shareholder loan (to be repaid within one year of the corporate year-end) or included on a T4 slip as a taxable benefit. Any other treatment invites reassessment and potential double taxation.

3. Ignoring Instalment Obligations

Both personal and corporate taxpayers may be required to pay quarterly tax instalments. If your net tax owing exceeds $3,000 ($1,800 for Quebec), you must pay by instalment. Missing or underpaying instalments results in non-deductible interest charges that can accumulate quickly at prescribed rates.

4. No Record of Automobile Mileage

Whether you use the simplified method (flat per-kilometre rate) or the detailed method (actual expenses prorated by business use), the CRA requires a logbook. A calendar-year logbook is valid for the year it covers. Without one, kilometre-based claims are likely to be denied on audit.

5. Over-contributing to a TFSA or RRSP

TFSA over-contributions incur a 1% monthly penalty on the excess. RRSP over-contributions beyond the $2,000 lifetime buffer incur a 1% monthly penalty. Always verify your available room through CRA My Account before making large deposits, especially if you have made withdrawals during the year.

6. Missing the December 31 Deadline for Tax Planning

Most personal tax planning must be completed by December 31. RRSP contributions are an exception (deadline is 60 days after year-end), but virtually everything else—tax-loss selling, charitable donations, medical expense timing, and spousal loan interest—needs to happen before December 31.

For corporations, bonuses must be declared and documented before the corporate year-end to be deductible in that year, and paid within 179 days to avoid a deemed dividend rule.

7. DIY Filings on Complex Situations

Tax software handles simple T4 employment well, but situations involving self-employment, rental properties, foreign income, or corporate structures benefit from professional review. The cost of a tax professional is often a fraction of the tax saved from proper planning.

The Best Time to Fix a Mistake Is Now

If you discover an error on a prior-year return, you can request an adjustment using a T1-ADJ form (or through CRA My Account). Voluntary disclosures are also available for more significant omissions. Proactive correction is always better than waiting for a CRA review.
