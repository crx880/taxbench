Tax Tips

Practical Tax Tips for Canadians

Actionable strategies, common credits, and planning reminders for the 2026 tax year.

Claim all your medical expenses — and time them well

Medical expenses can be claimed for any 12-month period ending in the tax year, not just the calendar year. If you had large expenses in early 2025, you may be able to include them in a period ending in 2026. Track premiums, prescriptions, dental work, glasses, and travel more than 40 km for medical care. The threshold is the lesser of $2,753 or 3% of net income.

Don’t overlook the Canada Workers Benefit (CWB)

If your working income is below roughly $24,000 (single) or $30,000 (family), you may qualify for this refundable credit. The CWB is not automatic—you must file a tax return and complete Schedule 6. You may also be eligible for advance payments through the ACWB program.

Keep corporate records and minute books current

For incorporated professionals, a current minute book is not optional. Dividend declarations, shareholder loans, and salary/bonus resolutions all need proper documentation. CRA can recharacterize transactions that lack supporting corporate resolutions. Review your minute book at least annually.

File on time even if you cannot pay

The late-filing penalty is 5% of the balance owing plus 1% per month, up to 12 months. If you have filed late in any of the three prior years, the penalty doubles. File on time—even if you cannot pay the full amount—to avoid the penalty. CRA will work with you on a payment arrangement.

Use tax-loss selling before December 31

If you have non-registered investments with unrealized losses, consider selling them before December 31 to realize the capital loss. The loss can offset capital gains realized in the current year, or any of the three prior years, or be carried forward indefinitely. Be mindful of the superficial loss rule if you repurchase within 30 days.

Maximize your Registered Disability Savings Plan (RDSP)

If you or a family member qualifies for the Disability Tax Credit, an RDSP can generate government grants and bonds of up to $3,500 per year on contributions. The lifetime grant limit is $70,000, and the bond maximum is $20,000. Contributions are not deductible, but growth is tax-deferred.

Split pension income with your spouse

If you receive eligible pension income (including RRIF payments if you are 65 or older, or lifetime annuity payments from a registered pension plan at any age), you can allocate up to 50% to your spouse or common-law partner. This can significantly reduce your combined tax bill if you are in a higher bracket.

Review payroll remittance frequency

For corporations with employees, CRA assigns a remittance frequency based on your average monthly withholding amount. If your payroll has decreased, you may qualify for a lower frequency (e.g., quarterly instead of monthly). An incorrect frequency can result in penalties even if the total annual amount is correct.

Carry forward unused tuition credits

Unused federal tuition credits carry forward indefinitely until you have tax to offset them against. You can also transfer up to $5,000 of current-year tuition credits to a parent, grandparent, or spouse. Keep your T2202 forms accessible—CRA may request them years later.

Contribute to an RESP for the Canada Education Savings Grant

The CESG matches 20% of your RESP contributions, up to $500 per year per beneficiary, with a lifetime limit of $7,200. The grant is effectively a 20% risk-free return. Unused grant room carries forward one year at a time, up to $1,000 of grant per year.
